Chapter 5 · Concept 1

Patterns, Parity & Multiples

Use algebra to describe number patterns, decide when an expression is always even or odd, and reason about multiples — the heart of this chapter's exam questions.

Key Concept Three big ideas

1. Consecutive numbers in algebra

If the smallest is n, consecutive numbers are n, n+1, n+2, … Naming one of them with a letter lets you describe the rest.

If the greatest of five consecutive numbers is p, the five are p, p−1, p−2, p−3, p−4.

2. Parity — when is an expression always even / odd?

•
If 2 is a factor of every term (e.g. 2a + 2b = 2(a+b), or 4x + 6 = 2(2x+3)), the value is always even.
•
The product of two consecutive numbers, n(n+1), is always even (one of them must be even). So is n² − n = n(n−1).
•
An expression like 2n + 1 is always odd; one like 3g + 5h is sometimes even, sometimes odd — it depends on the values.

3. Multiples and the LCM rule

•
If a divides M and a divides N, then a divides M + N and M − N.
•
If A is divisible by both k and m, then A is divisible by LCM(k, m). So divisible by 9 and 4 ⇒ divisible by 36. But divisible by 6 and 4 ⇒ divisible by 12 (LCM), not always 24.
•
To count multiples of m in a range, divide the endpoints by m. Multiples of 7 from 200 to 300: 7×29 = 203 up to 7×42 = 294, so 42 − 29 + 1 = 14.
Prerequisite refresher: "Parity" = whether a number is even or odd. A number is even exactly when 2 is one of its factors. "LCM" = lowest common multiple.

Common Traps Where marks slip away

Trap 1 — "Always" vs "sometimes". 2a + 2b is always even; 3g + 5h is only sometimes even. Check whether 2 is a factor of the whole expression.
Trap 2 — LCM, not the product. Divisible by 6 and 4 means divisible by LCM(6,4) = 12, not 6 × 4 = 24.
Trap 3 — Counting multiples in a range. Use (last index − first index + 1); don't just divide the range width.
Trap 4 — 1 is not prime. A prime has exactly two factors; 1 has only one, so 1 is not prime.
Slip 5 — Justify with algebra (e.g. 4x + 6 = 2(2x + 3)) to earn full marks on "always/sometimes/never" questions.

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. The greatest of five consecutive numbers is p. Write the other four.
Step 1:Consecutive numbers go down by 1 each step from p.
Step 2:The four below p are p−1, p−2, p−3, p−4.
∴ p−1, p−2, p−3, p−4
Example B. Show that 4x + 6 is always even.
Step 1:Take out the common factor 2: 4x + 6 = 2(2x + 3).
Step 2:Since 2 is a factor, the value is a multiple of 2 for every integer x.
∴ 4x + 6 is always even
Example C. The sum of three consecutive even numbers is 72. Find them.
Step 1:Let them be n, n+2, n+4. Sum = 3n + 6 = 72.
Step 2:3n = 66, so n = 22.
Step 3:The numbers are 22, 24, 26.
∴ 22, 24, 26
Example D. How many multiples of 7 lie between 200 and 300?
Step 1:Smallest multiple ≥ 200 is 7 × 29 = 203; largest ≤ 300 is 7 × 42 = 294.
Step 2:Count = 42 − 29 + 1 = 14.
∴ 14 multiples
