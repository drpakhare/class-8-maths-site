Cube Numbers

A cube number is a number multiplied by itself three times. Learn the cubes table, the digit patterns, the prime-factorisation test, and the "smallest number to multiply/divide for a perfect cube" problems.

Key Concept What is a cube number?

The idea: Multiplying a number by itself three times gives a cube number (or perfect cube). We write it with a small raised 3.
5 × 5 × 5 = 53 = 125    (a cube of side 5 is made of 125 unit cubes)

The cubes you should know by heart:

n12345678910
n³1827641252163435127291000

Also useful: 11³ = 1331, 12³ = 1728, 13³ = 2197, 15³ = 3375.

The patterns that get tested

1.
Last-digit rule. Unlike squares, a cube can end in any digit 0–9. The units digit of n³ depends only on the units digit of n:  1→1, 2→8, 3→7, 4→4, 5→5, 6→6, 7→3, 8→2, 9→9, 0→0.
2.
Trailing zeros come in 3s. A perfect cube can only end in a number of zeros that is a multiple of 3 (3, 6, …). So a cube can never end in exactly two zeros.
3.
Sum of consecutive odd numbers. 1 = 1³; 3 + 5 = 8 = 2³; 7 + 9 + 11 = 27 = 3³; 13 + 15 + 17 + 19 = 64 = 4³. The n-th group of odd numbers adds to n³.
4.
Perfect-cube test (prime factorisation). Group the prime factors in threes. If every prime forms complete triples with none left over, the number is a perfect cube.
Smallest number to multiply / divide: Whatever primes are short of a triple, multiply by them to complete the triples (or divide to remove the extras) — a classic 3-mark question.
Famous fact — 1729 (the Hardy–Ramanujan number): it is the smallest number expressible as the sum of two cubes in two ways: 1729 = 1³ + 12³ = 9³ + 10³.
Prerequisite refresher: "three times" means n × n × n, not n × 3. So 4³ = 64, not 12.

Common Traps Where marks slip away

Trap 1 — Cube ≠ × 3. 4³ = 4 × 4 × 4 = 64, not 12. This is the single most common cube error.
Trap 2 — Using the square rule for cubes. Cubes can end in 2, 3, 7, 8 — the "forbidden digits" rule is for squares only.
Trap 3 — Grouping in pairs. For cubes you group primes in threes, not pairs. Pairs are for square roots.
Trap 4 — Two trailing zeros. A cube cannot end in exactly two zeros — zeros must come in multiples of three.
Slip 5 — Notation. Write the index as a small raised 3: 5³. Don't write "53".

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. Is 9 a perfect cube?
Step 1:List nearby cubes: 2³ = 8 and 3³ = 27.
Step 2:9 lies between 8 and 27, so it is no whole number cubed.
∴ 9 is NOT a perfect cube
Example B. Find the units digit of the cube of 23.
Step 1:Only the units digit of 23 matters, which is 3.
Step 2:3³ = 27, which ends in 7.
∴ Units digit of 23³ = 7
Example C. Is 3375 a perfect cube? Use prime factorisation.
Step 1:3375 = 3 × 3 × 3 × 5 × 5 × 5.
Step 2:Group in threes: (3 × 3 × 3) × (5 × 5 × 5).
Step 3:Both primes form complete triples, so 3375 is a perfect cube.
∴ 3375 is a perfect cube (= 15³)
Example D. By what smallest number should 3600 be multiplied to make a perfect cube?
Step 1:3600 = 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 3 × 3 × 5 × 5 = 2⁴ × 3² × 5².
Step 2:For a cube, every exponent must be a multiple of 3. Currently 2⁴ (need 2 more 2s), 3² (need 1 more 3), 5² (need 1 more 5).
Step 3:Multiply by 2² × 3 × 5 = 4 × 3 × 5 = 60.
Check:3600 × 60 = 216000 = 60³.
∴ Multiply by 60
Example E. By what smallest number should 128 be divided to make a perfect cube?
Step 1:128 = 2⁷.
Step 2:The nearest lower multiple of 3 for the exponent is 6, so remove one 2.
Step 3:128 ÷ 2 = 64 = 2⁶ = 4³.
∴ Divide by 2 (quotient 64 = 4³)
