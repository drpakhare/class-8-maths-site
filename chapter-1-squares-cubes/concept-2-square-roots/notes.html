Square Roots

The square root is the reverse of squaring. Learn the symbol, the prime-factorisation method, how to estimate roots of non-perfect squares, and the "smallest number to multiply/divide" problems that show up for 3 marks.

Key Concept What is a square root?

The idea: Squaring a number and taking its square root are opposite operations. If y = x², then x is the square root of y. The symbol is √.
7² = 49  ⟺  √49 = 7   (a square of area 49 has side 7)

Positive and negative roots

Because 8 × 8 = 64 and (−8) × (−8) = 64, every perfect square has two square roots — one positive, one negative.

√64 = ± 8   |   in this chapter we use only the positive root, so we write √64 = 8

Method 1 — Square root by prime factorisation

Break the number into prime factors, pair identical primes, and take one factor from each pair. Their product is the square root.

Quick test for a perfect square: if every prime factor can be grouped into pairs with none left over, the number is a perfect square.

Method 2 — Repeated subtraction (small numbers)

Subtract 1, 3, 5, 7, … until you reach 0. The count of subtractions is the square root (covered in Concept 1).

Estimating the root of a non-perfect square

Trap it between the two nearest perfect squares you know, then narrow down. (Useful when the number is not a perfect square.)

Smallest number to multiply / divide: If one prime is left unpaired, multiplying or dividing by that prime fixes the pairing and makes a perfect square — a common 3-mark question.
Prerequisite refresher: Prime factorisation = writing a number as a product of primes (e.g. 36 = 2 × 2 × 3 × 3). A prime has exactly two factors: 1 and itself.

Common Traps Where marks slip away

Trap 1 — Taking the whole pair. From a pair (3 × 3) you take one 3 for the root, not 3 × 3. √(3×3) = 3.
Trap 2 — Forgetting a leftover prime. If a prime can't be paired, the number is not a perfect square. Don't force it.
Trap 3 — "Multiply" vs "divide". To remove a single leftover prime you can multiply (to add its pair) or divide (to delete it). Read the question — it usually asks for the smallest such number.
Trap 4 — Estimation direction. When estimating, state both neighbours (e.g. 15 < √250 < 16) and pick the closer one — don't just round randomly.
Slip 5 — The ± sign. Mention that perfect squares have ± roots, but use the positive root unless asked otherwise.

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. Find √11025 by prime factorisation.
Step 1:Prime factorise: 11025 = 3 × 3 × 5 × 5 × 7 × 7.
Step 2:Make pairs: (3 × 3) × (5 × 5) × (7 × 7).
Step 3:Take one from each pair: 3 × 5 × 7 = 105.
∴ √11025 = 105
Example B. Find √4761 by prime factorisation.
Step 1:4761 = 3 × 3 × 23 × 23.
Step 2:Pairs: (3 × 3) × (23 × 23).
Step 3:One from each pair: 3 × 23 = 69.
∴ √4761 = 69
Example C. By what smallest number should 2800 be multiplied to make it a perfect square? Find the new square root.
Step 1:2800 = 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 5 × 5 × 7.
Step 2:Pairs: (2 × 2)(2 × 2)(5 × 5) and a leftover 7 with no partner.
Step 3:Multiply by 7 to give 7 a pair: 2800 × 7 = 19600.
Step 4:√19600 = 2 × 2 × 5 × 7 = 140.
∴ Multiply by 7; √19600 = 140
Example D. By what smallest number should 216 be divided so the quotient is a perfect square? Find that square root.
Step 1:216 = 2 × 2 × 2 × 3 × 3 × 3.
Step 2:Pairs: (2 × 2) and (3 × 3); leftover 2 and 3 unpaired.
Step 3:Divide by the leftovers 2 × 3 = 6:   216 ÷ 6 = 36.
Step 4:√36 = 6.
∴ Divide by 6; quotient 36; √36 = 6
Example E. Estimate √250.
Step 1:Nearest squares: 15² = 225 and 16² = 256.
Step 2:So 15 < √250 < 16.
Step 3:256 is much closer to 250 than 225, so √250 ≈ 16 (just under 16).
∴ √250 ≈ 16
