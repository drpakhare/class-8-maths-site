Square Numbers & Their Properties

Read the key idea, learn the traps that cost marks, then study the worked examples — each one is written out line by line, exactly the way you should write it in the exam.

Key Concept What is a square number?

The idea: When a number is multiplied by itself, the result is a square number (also called a perfect square). We write it with a small raised 2.
5 × 5 = 52 = 25   →   read as "5 squared"

Why "square"? A square of side 5 contains exactly 5 × 5 = 25 unit squares. The area of a square is the square of its side.

The 4 properties that get tested

1.
Units-digit rule. A perfect square can only end in 0, 1, 4, 5, 6, or 9 — never in 2, 3, 7, or 8.
2.
Trailing-zeros rule. A perfect square always has an even number of zeros at the end (100 → 2 zeros, 10000 → 4). So 1000 (3 zeros) is not a square.
3.
Parity. The square of an even number is even; the square of an odd number is odd. (6²=36, 7²=49.)
4.
Sum of odd numbers. 1=1², 1+3=4=2², 1+3+5=9=3², 1+3+5+7=16=4². The sum of the first n odd numbers is n².
Two bonus facts examiners love:
• Count of numbers between n² and (n+1)² = 2n. (Between 25 and 36: 2×5 = 10.)
• Gap between consecutive squares n² and (n+1)² = 2n + 1.
Prerequisite refresher: "units digit" = the last digit; "parity" = whether a number is odd or even; "consecutive odd numbers" = 1, 3, 5, 7, … with no gaps.

Common Traps Where marks slip away

Trap 1 — Reversing the units-digit rule. Ending in 0,1,4,5,6,9 does not prove a number is a square. 26 ends in 6 but is not a square. Use the rule only to rule numbers out.
Trap 2 — "Square" ≠ "double". 5² = 25, not 10. Squaring is × itself.
Trap 3 — "How many between". Numbers strictly between n² and (n+1)² = 2n (not 2n+1, not n).
Trap 4 — Trailing zeros. Always count the zeros. An odd number of zeros (e.g. 1000) means it cannot be a square.
Slip 5 — Notation. Write the index small and raised: 7². Writing "72" loses the meaning and the mark.

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. Is 24 a perfect square?
Step 1:Check the units digit. 24 ends in 4, which is an allowed digit, so it is not ruled out yet.
Step 2:List nearby squares: 4² = 16 and 5² = 25. So 24 lies between two consecutive squares.
Step 3:Since 24 is not equal to any whole number squared, it is not a perfect square.
∴ 24 is NOT a perfect square
Example B. How many natural numbers lie between 5² and 6²?
Step 1:Use the rule: numbers between n² and (n+1)² = 2n.
Step 2:Here n = 5, so the count = 2 × 5 = 10.
Check:5² = 25 and 6² = 36. The numbers 26, 27, …, 35 number exactly 10.
∴ 10 numbers
Example C. Find the units digit of the square of 162.
Step 1:Only the units digit of the original number affects the units digit of the square.
Step 2:The units digit of 162 is 2, and 2² = 4.
Step 3:So the square of 162 ends in 4.
∴ Units digit = 4
Example D. Show that 36 is a perfect square by repeated subtraction of odd numbers, and find its square root.
Method:Subtract the odd numbers 1, 3, 5, 7, … one at a time until the result is 0.
Working:36 − 1 = 35;   35 − 3 = 32;   32 − 5 = 27;   27 − 7 = 20;   20 − 9 = 11;   11 − 11 = 0.
Count:We subtracted 6 odd numbers to reach exactly 0.
Conclude:Reaching 0 means 36 is a perfect square, and the count (6) is its square root.
∴ 36 is a perfect square and √36 = 6
Example E. Given 35² = 1225, find 36² using the odd-number pattern.
Step 1:The gap between n² and (n+1)² is (n) + (n+1). Here that is 35 + 36 = 71.
Step 2:So 36² = 35² + 71 = 1225 + 71.
Step 3:1225 + 71 = 1296.
∴ 36² = 1296
